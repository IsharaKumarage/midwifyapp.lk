Research Domain

Literature, gaps, objectives, methodology and technology at the core of our research.

Domain · 01

Literature Survey

The application of machine learning to maternal healthcare has gained significant traction. Assaduzzaman et al. (2023) demonstrated that ensemble classifiers such as Random Forest and XGBoost outperform traditional logistic regression in predicting maternal risk levels using routine vital signs. Harnal et al. (2025) explored SVMs for classifying gestational complications, achieving accuracy rates exceeding 91% on South Asian clinical datasets — directly underpinning our first research component.

Infant cranial health monitoring has also seen advances through AI computer vision. Nakahari et al. (2023) established quantitative indices — the Cranial Vault Asymmetry Index (CVAI) and Cephalic Index (CI) — as reliable markers for detecting plagiocephaly in newborns. CNN-based 2D photographic analysis has demonstrated diagnostic accuracy comparable to clinical gold standards, yet no bilingual, smartphone-accessible tool exists for non-specialist caregivers.

Fetal health monitoring via Cardiotocography (CTG) remains a cornerstone of intrapartum care. Tabatabaei et al. (2023) found that gradient boosting and neural network approaches significantly reduced false-negative rates in detecting fetal distress. The Ayres-de-Campos (2000) benchmark dataset of 2,126 CTG records underpins our third component's training pipeline, enabling Normal/Suspect/Pathological classification.

Virtual Reality for medical skills training outperforms mannequin-based training in skill transfer, retention, and learner confidence — particularly for neonatal procedures. Our fourth component translates these findings into a Unity-based OpenXR training environment validated through System Usability Scale (SUS) assessments.

Key References

  • Assaduzzaman et al. (2023) – ML for maternal risk
  • Harnal et al. (2025) – SVM in gestational complications
  • Nakahari et al. (2023) – CVAI & CI cranial indices
  • Tabatabaei et al. (2023) – CTG classification review
  • Ayres-de-Campos et al. (2000) – CTG benchmark dataset
Domain · 02

Research Gap

📵

No Midwife-Focused ML Apps

Existing maternal risk tools target hospital clinicians, not community-level midwives operating with limited infrastructure.

📊

No Real-Time Risk Visualization

Current systems lack intuitive dashboards that translate ML outputs into actionable clinical guidance for field practitioners.

🔔

No Voice or Haptic Alerts

Alert mechanisms are visual-only; no voice or haptic notification system exists for hands-free midwifery workflows.

🌐

No Bilingual (Sinhala/Tamil) Tools

All identified health applications are English-medium, excluding Sri Lanka's Sinhala- and Tamil-speaking population.

📡

No Offline-Capable AI for Rural Sri Lanka

Cloud-dependent AI tools are impractical in rural areas where internet access is unreliable. No offline-first ML deployment exists.

🥽

No Integrated VR + Postnatal Tools

No single platform combines immersive VR newborn-handling training with a postnatal care companion app.

Domain · 03

Research Problem

Sri Lankan midwives — who serve as the primary maternal healthcare touchpoint for a large proportion of the country's rural population — currently operate without intelligent, real-time digital decision-support tools. Critical clinical judgments including maternal risk stratification, infant cranial health assessment, fetal distress detection, and newborn handling training are executed through manual, experience-dependent processes prone to variability, delay, and error. No locally adapted, bilingual, offline-capable digital platform accounts for the unique linguistic, infrastructural, and clinical conditions of Sri Lanka's midwifery sector.

Research Question

How can integrated digital technologies — including ML, computer vision, mobile development, and VR — be effectively designed, developed, and evaluated to enhance midwives' decision-making capacity and care delivery in the Sri Lankan healthcare context?

Core Challenge

Midwives lack real-time intelligent tools for detecting maternal and fetal risks.

Context

Rural Sri Lanka, limited connectivity, bilingual population, primary care setting.

Proposed Solution

Four integrated digital tools covering risk prediction, infant monitoring, CTG assessment, and VR training.

Domain · 04

Objectives

Main Objective

To design, develop, and evaluate four integrated digital tools — leveraging ML, AI computer vision, mobile technology, and VR — that enhance the clinical decision-making capacity, diagnostic accuracy, and practical training of midwives within the Sri Lankan maternal healthcare system.

📱

Component 1 – Maternal Risk ML

  • Collect and preprocess a Sri Lankan maternal clinical dataset
  • Train Random Forest and XGBoost for risk classification
  • Deploy via TensorFlow Lite on a Flutter mobile app
  • Implement real-time visualization with voice/haptic alerts
  • Evaluate accuracy and usability with midwifery practitioners
👶

Component 2 – Infant Cranial AI

  • Build a CNN-based infant head image classification model
  • Implement CVAI and CI metric computation from 2D photos
  • Develop a bilingual Sinhala/Tamil React Native application
  • Integrate posture assessment functionality
  • Validate with paediatric experts and caregivers
🫀

Component 3 – CTG Assessment

  • Preprocess the Ayres-de-Campos CTG benchmark dataset
  • Train SVM and ensemble classifiers for 3-class fetal status
  • Expose model via a Flask REST API backend
  • Build a dashboard with Chart.js visualizations
  • Evaluate against clinical baselines
🥽

Component 4 – VR Training

  • Model a newborn handling simulation in Unity/Blender
  • Implement OpenXR-compatible VR interactions
  • Integrate Google Speech API for voice instructions
  • Develop a companion postnatal Flutter mobile app
  • Conduct SUS testing with 15–20 midwifery students
Domain · 05

Methodology

The research employs an iterative, human-centred development methodology. Structured requirement-gathering sessions are conducted with practising midwives, midwifery educators, and healthcare administrators. Semi-structured interviews and observational workflows are analyzed to identify priority clinical pain points and translate them into precise functional and non-functional requirements for each of the four components.

Dataset collection and preprocessing form the technical foundation. Maternal records are aggregated, cleaned using Pandas, and balanced via SMOTE oversampling. A curated infant photographic dataset is labeled by paediatric specialists and augmented through rotation and brightness variation. The Ayres-de-Campos CTG dataset serves as the benchmark for the fetal health component. All ML models — Random Forest, CNN — are trained in TensorFlow and compressed to TFLite for on-device offline inference.

Development follows Flutter and React Native frameworks for mobile, with Firebase providing authentication and real-time sync. The VR component is built in Unity 2023 LTS with OpenXR, modeled in Blender, and tested across Meta Quest and PC VR headsets. Summative SUS evaluation involves 15–20 midwifery students from an affiliated healthcare institution.

Phase 1 · Requirements

Midwife interviews, workflow analysis, stakeholder mapping, feature specification.

Phase 2 · Data & Modelling

Dataset collection, preprocessing (Pandas, SMOTE), model training (RF, CNN, SVM, TFLite).

Phase 3 · Development

Flutter & React Native apps, Flask API, Unity/OpenXR VR environment, Firebase integration.

Phase 4 · Evaluation

SUS usability testing, ML accuracy benchmarking, expert clinical review, final iteration.
